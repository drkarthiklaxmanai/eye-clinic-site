One of the most common questions before cataract surgery isn't about the procedure itself, but about what comes after -- how quickly vision improves, what restrictions apply, and when life can return to normal.

## Day of Surgery

Cataract surgery is performed as day-care surgery -- you walk in and walk out the same day. The procedure itself typically takes under 30 minutes, performed under topical (drop) anesthesia. You'll rest briefly afterward and head home with a protective shield over the eye and instructions for your first dose of prescribed eye drops.

## Day 1

Most patients notice a dramatic improvement in vision within 24 hours. Mild grittiness, light sensitivity, or watering are common and expected -- these settle quickly. Your first follow-up visit is usually scheduled around this point to confirm everything looks as expected.

## Days 2-7

Vision continues to stabilize and sharpen over this first week. You'll continue using prescribed anti-inflammatory and antibiotic eye drops on a tapering schedule. It's normal for vision to fluctuate slightly day to day during this period. Light activity is generally fine, but bending, heavy lifting, and swimming are usually best avoided.

## Weeks 2-4

By this point, most of the visual recovery is complete, though the eye continues fine-tuning its healing during this window. A second follow-up visit typically happens around 2-4 weeks to confirm healing is on track and, if needed, finalize any updated glasses prescription for residual near or distance correction depending on the lens chosen.

## After 4 Weeks

The eye is generally considered fully healed by this stage. If a new pair of glasses is needed (common with monofocal lenses, less common with multifocal/trifocal lenses), this is usually when the final prescription is confirmed.

## A Few Things That Are Normal, Not Concerning

- Mild fluctuation in vision clarity during the first couple of weeks
- A slightly scratchy or gritty sensation for the first few days
- Some sensitivity to bright light initially

## When to Reach Out Sooner

Significant pain, a sudden decrease in vision after initial improvement, or increasing redness should be evaluated promptly rather than waiting for a scheduled follow-up -- though these are uncommon with modern small-incision surgery.

Every recovery is slightly individual, but this general timeline holds true for the vast majority of patients undergoing standard phacoemulsification cataract surgery.
