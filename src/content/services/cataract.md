A cataract is the clouding of the eye's natural lens, which lies behind the iris and the pupil. It is a normal part of aging, but it can lead to a progressive decrease in vision over time -- often so gradually that many people don't fully realize how much their vision has changed until it's pointed out during an examination, or until the improvement after surgery makes the difference obvious in hindsight.

Cataracts develop at different rates in different people, and there's no single "right time" that applies to everyone. Some patients choose to proceed once the cataract starts affecting daily activities like reading or night driving; others prefer to wait until the impact feels more significant. Both are reasonable approaches, and this is a decision we make together based on how the cataract is actually affecting your life, not simply how advanced it looks on examination.

## What to Expect at Your Visit

Your first visit focuses on confirming the diagnosis and understanding exactly how the cataract is affecting your vision. This typically includes a dilated examination of the lens, a visual acuity assessment, and biometry -- precise measurements used to calculate the correct power for your intraocular lens, should you proceed with surgery.

If surgery is appropriate, we'll walk through your lens options in detail at this stage, with enough time to ask questions before any decision is made. There's no pressure to decide immediately; many patients prefer to think it over and return with questions.

## The Procedure Itself

At Crispr Eye Care, cataract surgery uses micro-incision phacoemulsification -- a stitchless, painless procedure performed under topical (drop) anesthesia rather than injections. Through a microscopic incision, ultrasound energy is used to gently break up the cloudy natural lens, which is then removed and replaced with the intraocular lens (IOL) selected during your consultation.

The procedure itself typically takes well under 30 minutes, and most patients go home the same day. Because no stitches are required and the incision is so small, recovery is generally faster and more comfortable than many people expect going in.

## Choosing the Right Lens

This is one of the most important decisions in the process, since the lens you choose affects your day-to-day vision for years to come.

**Monofocal lenses** provide excellent, sharp vision at one distance -- usually far -- and remain a reliable, well-established choice. Reading glasses are typically still needed for near tasks.

**Multifocal and trifocal lenses** are designed to provide clear vision across multiple distances -- far, intermediate, and near -- meaningfully reducing dependence on glasses for most daily activities.

**Toric lenses** specifically correct astigmatism at the same time as the cataract, which can otherwise leave residual blur even after an otherwise successful surgery.

There isn't a universally "best" lens -- the right choice depends on your lifestyle, your visual priorities, and sometimes the health of other structures in your eye. We discuss this in detail during your evaluation, with realistic expectations about what each option can and can't do.

## Recovery and Follow-Up

Most patients notice a dramatic improvement in vision within 24 hours, though full visual stabilization can take a few weeks as the eye heals completely. A short course of eye drops is typically prescribed to support healing and reduce the risk of infection or inflammation. Follow-up visits allow us to confirm everything is healing as expected and address any questions that come up after you've had time to adjust to your new vision.
